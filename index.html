Letting the neural
code speak

Automated characterization of monkey visual neurons through human language. A closed-loop framework using vision–language models, digital twins, and generative image synthesis to translate neural selectivity into interpretable semantic descriptions.

Vedang Lad, Katrin Franke, Tamar Rott Shaham, Surya Ganguli, Andreas Tolias†, Sophia Sanborn†, Nikos Karantzas†

Stanford University · University of Tübingen · MIT    †Equal senior contribution

Approach

From neural activity to language and back

Modern encoding models predict neural responses with remarkable accuracy, yet prediction alone does not yield interpretation. We ask whether natural language can bridge this gap—translating the selectivity of individual neurons into concise, testable semantic descriptions, then verifying those descriptions through generative image synthesis.

01

Translate

Each image is converted into a dense textual description using Gemini 3.0 Pro, preserving fine-grained visual detail sufficient to reconstruct the original stimulus.

02

Hypothesize

For each neuron, a digital twin screens millions of images. Captions of extreme-response images are distilled into a concise semantic hypothesis describing the neuron's selectivity.

03

Verify

Hypotheses are converted into novel images via text-to-image generation. If generated images drive the neuron as predicted, the hypothesis is validated in closed loop.

Framework overview. The pipeline consists of three stages: Translate converts images to detailed captions, Semantic Hypothesis distills captions of extreme-response images into testable selectivity descriptions, and Verification generates novel stimuli from hypotheses and tests them against the digital twin.

Stage 1

Faithful image-to-text translation

We use dense captioning to convert each image into a multi-sentence description specifying precise colors, textures, spatial arrangements, and lighting conditions. A round-trip reconstruction test confirms that captions preserve visually relevant information: images synthesized from captions are consistently more similar to their originals than to unrelated images in DINO embedding space.

V4 caption faithfulness. Dense captions generated by Gemini 3.0 Pro preserve sufficient visual detail for reconstruction. Cosine similarity between original and reconstructed images in DINO space consistently exceeds the null baseline.

V1 caption faithfulness. The same analysis applied to grayscale V1 stimuli confirms that the captioning procedure generalizes across different image statistics regimes.

Stage 2

Semantic hypotheses across the visual hierarchy

Digital twin models screen over one million ImageNet images to identify each neuron's most and least activating stimuli. Dense captions of these extreme images are synthesized into concise semantic hypotheses. The pipeline reveals a clear progression: V1 hypotheses emphasize oriented edges and spatial frequency, while V4 hypotheses capture richer conjunctions of form, color, and texture—recovering known tuning properties without any injected domain knowledge.

V4 semantic hypotheses. For each neuron, top-activating images are identified via the digital twin and their captions are distilled into interpretable selectivity descriptions. Examples show diverse feature conjunctions including eye-like structures, curved metallic edges, and textured surfaces.

V1 semantic hypotheses. The same pipeline applied to V1 recovers the canonical vocabulary of early visual cortex—oriented edges, spatial frequency bands, and contrast polarity—serving as an internal positive control.

Stage 3

Closed-loop verification

Semantic hypotheses are converted into novel images via text-to-image generation, then tested against the digital twin. Combined with spatial optimization through affine transformations, hypothesis-generated images drove 96% of neurons above the 95th percentile of natural image responses across both V1 and V4.

V4 verification. Hypothesis-generated images resemble original most-activating stimuli and, after spatial optimization, drive neurons to extreme response percentiles. Control analysis with random images confirms that semantic content—not spatial search alone—is necessary for strong activation.

V1 verification. The pipeline generalizes to V1, though the smaller gain from spatial optimization quantifies the expected boundary of linguistic expressibility for sub-lexical features like orientation and spatial frequency.

Cross-modal alignment

Language preserves the geometry of neural selectivity

Representational similarity analysis across six embedding spaces reveals that neural activity, visual features, and language share a common geometric structure. Neural responses to hypothesis-generated images align with original neural activity at r = 0.52 in V4, demonstrating that the full translate–hypothesize–generate loop preserves neurally relevant selectivity structure.

V4 alignment. Representational Similarity Matrices across six embedding spaces show consistent block structure. Image–caption alignment is strongest (r = 0.66), and neural responses to hypothesis-generated images align with original neural activity at r = 0.52.

V1 alignment. The same six-way alignment structure holds in V1, though with weaker neural–generated alignment (r = 0.30 vs. 0.52 in V4), tracing the boundary of linguistic expressibility across the visual hierarchy.

Population structure

A semantic cartography of neural selectivity

Projecting V4 neurons into two dimensions via UMAP using population activity similarity, and annotating each neuron with keywords from its semantic hypothesis, reveals smooth semantic transitions across the population. Language does not merely label neural selectivity but preserves its organization.

Semantic structure. Left: UMAP embedding annotated with nouns and adjectives from each neuron's hypothesis shows smooth semantic transitions. Right: Individual neurons tile localized, semantically coherent regions with smoothly varying activation.
